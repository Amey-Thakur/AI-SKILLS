# Strong openings

The opening decides whether the rest gets read. A reader gives you one or
two sentences to prove the piece is worth their time; waste them on
windup and you lose readers who would have valued the payoff. The first
line does the most work of any line.

## Method

1. **Cut the warm-up.** Most drafts begin one or two paragraphs before the
   real start: background, throat-clearing, "In today's world". Delete
   them and start where it gets interesting. The opening you wrote to warm
   yourself up is rarely the one the reader needs.
2. **Open with the point, a hook, or a concrete image.** For informative
   writing, lead with the answer or the key finding (see
   exec-one-pager). For engagement, open a curiosity gap, make a bold
   claim, ask a sharp question, or drop the reader into a specific moment
   (see narrative-craft). Match the opening move to the piece's job.
3. **Make a promise and be specific.** The opening should tell the reader
   what they will get and why it matters to them, concretely. "Here is how
   to cut your build time in half" beats "Build times are an important
   topic." Specificity in the first line signals a piece worth reading
   (see headline-hooks prompt).
4. **Get to the value fast, then deliver it.** Even a great hook fails if
   the payoff never comes or takes too long. The opening writes a check
   the piece must cash; do not over-tease. Curiosity gaps that do not
   close, or hooks the content does not earn, feel like clickbait and burn
   trust.
5. **Lead with the strongest material, not the chronological start.** You
   need not begin at the beginning; begin at the most compelling entry
   point and fill in context as needed. The best opening is often
   something from the middle or end of the actual events.
6. **Write the opening last, or rewrite it.** You often do not know the
   real opening until the piece is done and you see what it became. Draft
   something to start, then come back and craft the opening to fit the
   finished piece (see editing-and-revision).

## Boundaries

- A strong opening cannot carry weak content; it raises expectations the
  body must meet, so an overhyped opener on a thin piece disappoints
  harder than a plain one.
- Genre sets the conventions: a news lead, a story's cold open, a report's
  summary, an email's first line each have a shape (see press-release,
  reply-to-email). Learn the form's opening move.
- The same discipline applies to titles, subject lines, and hooks, where
  the stakes are even higher because they decide whether the piece is
  opened at all (see headline-hooks, newsletter).
