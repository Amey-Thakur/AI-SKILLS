# Narrative craft

A story sticks where a summary slides off, because the reader lives it
rather than being told it. The craft is the same whether the story is a
paragraph in an essay or a whole piece: a character wanting something,
something in the way, and a change.

## Method

1. **Anchor on a character who wants something.** The reader connects to a
   person (or a stand-in: a customer, a team, "you") with a goal and a
   stake. Without someone to care about and something they want, you have
   a report, not a story. State the want early.
2. **Introduce tension: the obstacle.** The engine of every story is a
   problem, conflict, or obstacle between the character and their goal.
   Stories without tension are announcements ("we launched, it went
   great"). The struggle is the interesting part; do not skip to the win.
3. **Show through concrete scenes, do not summarize.** A specific moment
   (the deploy at 2am, the customer's exact words) makes the reader
   experience it; "we faced challenges" makes them experience nothing.
   Sensory, specific detail is what turns telling into showing (see
   writing-with-evidence's specificity, aimed at emotion).
4. **Build an arc: setup, turn, resolution.** Establish the situation and
   the want, hit the turning point (the crisis, the insight, the change),
   land the resolution and what changed. The change is the payoff; a story
   where nothing shifts leaves the reader asking "so what".
5. **Land the point through the story, not bolted on.** The meaning should
   emerge from what happened, felt rather than lectured. A moral stapled
   to the end ("and that's why teamwork matters") deflates it; trust the
   story to carry the point (see story-narrative prompt for applying this).
6. **Cut to the tension.** Start close to the interesting moment, not with
   throat-clearing backstory. Give only the setup the payoff needs. A
   tight story that starts late and ends on the turn beats a complete one
   that meanders.

## Boundaries

- Narrative is a tool for making a point memorable and felt; it serves the
  message, so keep it pointed, not self-indulgent. A story that does not
  earn its length in a working document is padding.
- Honesty binds: use real detail where you have it, mark invented
  illustration as such, and never fabricate a customer, quote, or event
  as if real (see the ethics in case-study, story-narrative).
- Not every piece wants a story; a reference, a spec, or a quick update is
  worse for one. Reach for narrative where emotional truth or memorability
  is the goal (see technical-writing for the opposite).
