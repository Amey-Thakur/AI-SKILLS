# Executive briefing

An executive reads your brief between two other meetings and needs the decision,
not the journey. The common failure is a slow build: background, then analysis,
then finally the ask on page four, by which point the reader has skimmed to the
end looking for it anyway. Lead with the conclusion and earn the rest of their
attention with it.

## Method

1. **Open with the answer, in the first two sentences.** State the recommendation
   or the status, then the one number that proves it. "We should delay the launch
   two weeks; the payments path fails one in twenty test transactions." Everything
   after this exists to support a conclusion the reader already has.
2. **Fit it on one page.** If it does not fit, you have not decided what matters
   yet. One page forces the ruthless cut between what the executive needs and what
   you are proud of having found. Detail goes in an appendix they can open if they
   choose to.
3. **Pair every risk with an ask.** A risk without a request is a worry you handed
   upward; a risk with a request is a decision you enabled. "Vendor may miss the
   date (risk); approve the backup contract by Friday (ask)." Executives exist to
   unblock, so give them the specific lever to pull.
4. **Kill the surprises before the meeting.** Pre-brief anyone who would be blindsided
   or would object in the room: your skip-level, the affected peer, finance. A
   briefing is where a decision is ratified, not where a fight starts. If someone
   will push back hard, they should have already pushed back in private.
5. **Quantify, and show the range.** Replace "significant delay" with "three to
   five weeks, most likely four." Executives calibrate on numbers and distrust
   adjectives. When you are uncertain, state the range and the assumption driving
   it rather than hiding behind a soft word.
6. **End with what you need and by when.** Close on the decision, the owner, and
   the date. "Need go/no-go on the migration by end of week to hold the Q3 date."
   A brief that trails off into "happy to discuss" wasted the one chance to get a
   commitment.

## Litmus tests

- If the reader stopped after the first paragraph, would they have the decision?
- Does every risk name the specific action you want taken about it?
- Is there anyone who would be surprised in the room, and have you pre-briefed them?

## Boundaries

This is the format for a decision or status brief, not a strategy narrative that
argues a case over several pages: for that reach for the six-pager-narrative
skill. Norms on length and pre-read culture vary by company and by executive;
learn what your specific audience reads and rewards.
