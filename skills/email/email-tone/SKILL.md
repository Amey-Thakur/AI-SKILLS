# Email tone

Email strips out voice, face, and pause, so the reader supplies the tone
from the words alone, and they tend to read neutral as cold and brief as
curt. Getting tone right means writing a little warmer than feels
necessary, and matching the register to the reader and the moment.

## Method

1. **Write warmer than neutral.** Because email flattens tone, a message
   that feels neutral to you can read as cold or annoyed to the reader. A
   warm greeting, a "thanks", and a friendly close cost little and prevent
   misreads. Add a touch of warmth, especially when the content is a request
   or a correction.
2. **Match the register to the relationship and context.** A first email to
   a senior stranger is more formal; a note to a close colleague is casual.
   Mirror the other person's register (if they are warm and informal, match
   it; if formal, match that) rather than defaulting to stiff or breezy
   (see audience-adaptation, voice-and-tone).
3. **Match the tone to the situation.** Celebratory news is warm and
   enthusiastic; a serious or corrective message is calm and respectful;
   bad news is direct but kind (see difficult-emails). Tone-deaf mismatches
   (jokey during a problem, cold during good news) undercut the message.
4. **Soften requests and corrections without weakening them.** "Could you
   send the report by Friday?" lands better than "Send the report by
   Friday", while still being clear. Politeness and directness coexist; the
   goal is a clear ask wrapped in courtesy, not a vague one drowned in
   hedging.
5. **Cut the tone-killers.** Sarcasm and irony (they do not survive text),
   ALL CAPS (reads as shouting), excessive exclamation points (reads as
   forced), and passive-aggressive phrasing ("per my last email", "as I
   already mentioned") that signals irritation. If you are annoyed, wait
   before sending.
6. **Reread as the recipient before sending.** Read it imagining you are
   the reader having a bad day: does anything land as cold, blaming, or
   demanding? The five-second reread catches the sentence that would have
   caused a needless friction. When in doubt on a sensitive email, sleep on
   it.

## Boundaries

- Warmth does not mean padding or vagueness; keep the message clear and the
  ask explicit (see clear-emails). Tone is how you say it, not a reason to
  bury what you are saying.
- Cultural and individual norms for warmth and formality vary widely; when
  unsure, a moderately warm, professional tone is the safe default, and
  the reader's own style is the best guide.
- Some conversations should not be emails at all: real conflict, sensitive
  feedback, or anything emotionally charged is better in person or by call,
  where tone is not left to the reader's imagination (see giving-feedback).
