# Email follow-ups

Most emails that go unanswered were not refused; they were missed, buried,
or deprioritized by a busy person. A good follow-up gently resurfaces the
ask and makes it easy to act, without the passive-aggression or nagging
that turns a non-reply into a no.

## Method

1. **Wait a reasonable interval.** Give the reader time before following up:
   a few business days for most work email, longer for lower-priority asks,
   shorter only when there is a genuine deadline. Following up the next
   morning reads as impatient; the reader was likely just busy.
2. **Assume good faith, always.** Write as if they simply missed it (they
   usually did), not as if they are ignoring you. Warm and light, never
   accusatory. "Just floating this back to the top of your inbox" beats
   "You haven't responded to my email" (see email-tone).
3. **Add value, do not just bump.** A follow-up that only says "any update?"
   gives the reader nothing new to act on. Add something: restate the ask
   concisely (they should not have to scroll), a new piece of information, a
   narrowed request, an approaching deadline, or making it easier to say
   yes (see follow-up-email prompt).
4. **Make replying effortless.** Include the original context so they need
   not dig, and reduce the ask to the smallest possible action (a yes/no, a
   pick between two options). The easier you make the reply, the faster it
   comes.
5. **Reply within the same thread.** Follow up on the existing thread (not a
   new email) so the reader has the full context and history in one place,
   with the same subject or a lightly updated one (see email-etiquette's
   threading).
6. **Know when to stop, and offer an out.** After a couple of follow-ups
   with no response, the answer is effectively no or not-now; a final
   message offering an easy out ("if this is not a priority right now, no
   problem, just let me know") is graceful and often prompts the reply.
   Beyond that, escalating annoyance costs the relationship for little gain.

## Boundaries

- Follow-up cadence scales with stakes: a critical deadline warrants
  closer, clearer follow-ups (and possibly a call); a nice-to-have does
  not justify repeated chasing.
- Persistence and pestering differ by tone and value-add, not by count: a
  second follow-up that helps the reader act is fine; a fifth "just
  checking in" is nagging. Judge by whether each message earns its
  interruption.
- If email follow-ups are not working and it matters, change the channel:
  a quick call or message often resolves in minutes what three emails
  could not (see async-communication's medium choice).
