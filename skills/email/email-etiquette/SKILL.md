# Email etiquette

Email etiquette is the set of small conventions that mark you as
considerate and professional, and whose violation quietly costs goodwill.
The rules are mostly about respecting other people's time and attention:
who you copy, when you reply-all, and how fast you respond.

## Method

1. **Use To, CC, and BCC deliberately.** To is for the people who must act;
   CC is for those who need to be aware but need not respond; BCC is for
   privacy (hiding a large recipient list) or discreetly dropping someone
   from a thread, not for secretly looping in third parties (which
   backfires when they reply-all). Copying people needlessly buries them in
   noise.
2. **Think before reply-all.** Reply only to the sender unless everyone on
   the thread genuinely needs your message. "Thanks!" or "+1" to twenty
   people is the classic reply-all annoyance. When you do reply-all,
   confirm the recipient list is still appropriate.
3. **Respect response-time norms.** Reply within the expectation for the
   context (same-day for most work email, faster for urgent, and set an
   out-of-office when away; see async-communication). If a full answer will
   take time, a quick "got it, will reply by Thursday" prevents the sender
   from wondering (see email-follow-ups from the other side).
4. **Keep threads coherent.** Reply within the existing thread for a
   continuing conversation (so context stays together), and start a new
   thread with a fresh subject when the topic changes (see
   email-subject-lines). Do not hijack an unrelated thread for a new topic;
   the reader loses the history.
5. **Mind tone and permanence.** Email lacks vocal cues, so neutral can
   read as cold and a joke can misfire; err toward warmth and clarity (see
   email-tone). And remember email is forwardable and permanent: do not
   write what you would not want seen widely, and never handle a heated
   conflict over email.
6. **Handle attachments and links considerately.** Mention the attachment
   in the body (and actually attach it: the forgotten-attachment follow-up
   is universal), keep files reasonably sized or share a link, and give
   links context so the reader knows what they are clicking (link safety
   matters to them too).

## Boundaries

- Norms vary by culture, company, and relationship: some workplaces are
  formal, others chat-casual; read the room and mirror the sender's
  register rather than applying one rulebook (see email-tone,
  audience-adaptation).
- Etiquette is about consideration, not rigid formality; a quick casual
  reply to a close colleague needs none of the ceremony a first email to
  an executive does.
- These are conventions, not laws; the underlying principle (respect the
  recipient's time and attention) resolves the cases the rules do not
  cover.
