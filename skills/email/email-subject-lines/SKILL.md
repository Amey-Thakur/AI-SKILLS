# Email subject lines

The subject line is the email's first and often only impression. It decides
whether the reader opens it now, later, or never, and whether they can find
it again next week. A specific, honest subject is a small thing that
changes the response rate.

## Method

1. **Be specific about the content.** "Q3 budget: need your approval by
   Friday" tells the reader what it is and what it needs; "Following up" or
   "Quick question" tells them nothing and gets deprioritized. The reader
   should know the topic and roughly what is required from the subject
   alone.
2. **Front-load the key words.** Inboxes truncate subjects, especially on
   mobile; put the most important words first. "Invoice #482 overdue,
   payment needed" survives truncation better than "Just checking in about
   the invoice that..." where the point is cut off.
3. **Signal the action or urgency honestly.** If a response or a deadline is
   needed, say so ("[Action needed]", "by Thursday", "FYI, no reply
   needed"). This helps the reader triage correctly. But never fake urgency
   or importance to force an open; it works once, then trains the reader to
   distrust your subjects.
4. **Make it findable later.** Include the identifiers the reader (or you)
   would search for: a project name, an invoice number, a date. A subject
   that is searchable turns the email into a locatable record, not a lost
   one.
5. **Keep it short and skimmable.** A few words that capture the essence
   beat a full sentence. The subject is a label, not a summary; the body
   holds the detail (see clear-emails).
6. **Match the subject to the email.** A subject promising one thing over a
   body about another frustrates and erodes trust. If a thread has drifted
   to a new topic, change the subject (or start a new thread) so it stays
   accurate and findable.

## Boundaries

- Subject-line craft raises open and response rates; it cannot rescue a
  poorly written or purposeless email (see clear-emails). The subject gets
  it opened; the body has to deliver.
- Marketing/newsletter subject lines optimize for opens at scale with
  their own testing (see newsletter, headline-hooks); a one-to-one work
  email optimizes for clarity and honesty, not clickiness.
- Clever or curiosity-gap subjects that under-deliver read as clickbait in
  a professional context and damage credibility; specific and honest wins
  for real correspondence.
