# Inbox management

An inbox left to fill becomes a stressful pile where important things hide
among noise. Managing it is a simple discipline: process email in batches,
decide each one once, and keep the inbox as a short list of what actually
needs you, not a graveyard of everything ever received.

## Method

1. **Process in batches, not continuously.** Checking email constantly
   fragments your attention and lets it interrupt real work all day. Set a
   few dedicated times to process it, and close it in between (see the
   focus-protection in async-communication). Most email is not urgent; the
   truly urgent finds another channel.
2. **Decide each email once.** When you open an email, decide its fate then,
   rather than leaving it to re-read later: do it now if it takes two
   minutes, reply/act if longer, delegate if it is someone else's, defer
   with a clear next-action (flag or move to a "to-do" state), or delete/
   archive if it needs nothing. Reopening the same email repeatedly is the
   main time sink.
3. **Keep the inbox to what needs action.** Archive or file everything that
   is done or reference; the inbox should hold only what still requires
   something from you, so it functions as a short, honest task list. A
   thousand-item inbox hides the ten that matter (search finds reference
   mail; you do not need it cluttering the inbox).
4. **Triage by what actually matters, not what shouts.** Not every email
   deserves equal attention or a fast reply; distinguish the important
   (real consequences) from the merely loud, and handle accordingly (see
   prioritize-tasks' urgent-vs-important). Newsletters and CCs are not the
   same priority as a direct request from your boss.
5. **Reduce the inflow.** Unsubscribe from what you do not read, turn off
   non-essential notifications, filter or auto-file predictable mail
   (newsletters, alerts) out of the main inbox, and ask to be removed from
   threads you do not need. Less arriving is less to process.
6. **Set expectations so email does not own you.** Response-time norms (see
   email-etiquette), an out-of-office when away, and a quick "will reply
   by X" for things that need real time, all reduce the pressure to answer
   instantly and the anxiety of a full inbox. You control the inbox; it
   does not control you.

## Boundaries

- The specific system (inbox-zero, folders, flags, a particular app)
  matters less than the habits: process in batches, decide once, keep the
  inbox actionable. Adopt whatever tool supports those.
- Inbox management is personal productivity; it does not fix a culture that
  expects instant email replies, which needs a team-level norm conversation
  (see async-communication).
- Email is one channel among many (chat, docs, meetings); managing it well
  includes routing things to the right channel, not doing everything by
  email (see async-communication's medium choice).
