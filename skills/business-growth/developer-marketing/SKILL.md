# Developer marketing

Developers buy after trying, trust after verifying, and filter
marketing reflexively. The playbook inverts consumer marketing:
the docs are the campaign, the free tier is the sales call, and
credibility compounds while hype depreciates.

## Method

1. **Treat docs and onboarding as the funnel.** The
   quickstart that works in ten minutes (see
   tutorial-writing's tested-steps rule,
   readme-writing) converts better than any ad;
   friction logs from watching real first-runs (see
   user-activation's observation habit) are the
   marketing backlog. Public docs also do the SEO
   work (see technical-seo): developers search
   problems, and the tool whose docs answer them wins
   the evaluation before sales knows it started.
2. **Publish content that would survive your logo's
   removal.** Engineering postmortems (see
   incident-postmortem's candor: the genre developers
   share), benchmark methodology with reproducible
   numbers (see benchmark-design: gamed benchmarks are
   reputation arson), how-it-works internals, honest
   comparison pages that concede real tradeoffs.
   The test from conference-talks applies to every
   artifact: useful if the employer vanished?
   Content marketing that is marketing first ranks
   nowhere and converts nobody.
3. **Design the free tier as the evaluation path.**
   Generous enough to prove value on a real project
   (see saas-pricing's entry-tier rule), no credit
   card for hello-world, limits framed by scale rather
   than crippled features; self-serve from landing to
   working integration without a sales conversation
   (see landing-page-strategy's B2B boundary: the
   demo-gate belongs upstream, at enterprise).
4. **Earn community presence; never astroturf.** Answer
   questions where developers already are (forums,
   Stack Overflow, the framework's Discord) as helpful
   engineers with disclosed affiliation; sponsor the
   tools and events your users love; accept that
   community trust builds in years and burns in one
   sockpuppet scandal (see community-building for
   your own spaces; open-source-maintainer-role for
   the OSS edition of this contract).
5. **Measure influence, not just clicks.** Attribution
   is structurally weak (developers read anonymously,
   recommend in private channels, adopt months later):
   track leading indicators (docs traffic to key
   pages, quickstart completion, SDK downloads,
   community growth) alongside honest lagging revenue
   attribution surveys ("how did you hear about us"),
   and resist optimizing content toward what
   attributes over what convinces (see
   product-metrics' Goodhart warning, marketing
   edition).
6. **Let engineers speak, with support.** Developer
   audiences trust practitioners over marketers:
   engineering blog posts, conference talks (see
   conference-talks), livestreamed builds: supported
   by editing and amplification rather than ghostwritten
   into corporate voice (see writing-style instincts:
   the author's fingerprints are the credibility).

## Boundaries

- Developer marketing cannot rescue a bad developer
  experience; if the API is inconsistent (see
  api-design) or the docs wrong, marketing
  accelerates the discovery of that fact.
- The buyer and the developer diverge in enterprise;
  developer love opens doors that procurement still
  has to walk through: run both motions, do not
  confuse them (see saas-pricing's tier design).
- Hype cycles (launch-day virality, leaderboard
  stunts) spend trust for spikes; the compounding
  channel is boring consistency, which is a budget
  commitment leadership must actually make (see
  technical-seo's latency honesty).
