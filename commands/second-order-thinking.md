Think through the second- and third-order consequences of: {decision}

Context: {context}

Most decisions are judged on their first effect. The consequences that bite
live one and two steps further out. Trace them:

1. First-order: the immediate, intended effect. The obvious result.
2. Second-order: and then what? What does that first effect cause? How do
   people, incentives, and the system respond to it? (People adapt; markets
   react; what you optimize, someone games.)
3. Third-order: and then what? Where does the chain lead over time as the
   responses compound?
4. Look for the traps: effects that reverse the intent (the fix that creates a
   worse problem), incentives you accidentally create, who is affected that you
   did not consider, and what looks good now but costs later (or the reverse).
5. Ask "and then what?" until the chain stops being informative.

Finish with the net picture: does this still look good when you account for the
downstream effects, and what would you watch for or change to avoid the traps?

Rules: follow the causal chain concretely, grounded in how people and systems
actually respond (not a vague "there could be consequences"). Consider both
good and bad second-order effects. Be honest if the downstream analysis changes
the verdict on the decision. The value is seeing past the seductive first
effect: name the non-obvious consequence I am not seeing.
